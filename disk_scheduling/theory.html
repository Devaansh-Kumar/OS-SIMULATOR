Disk Scheduling Theory


First-Come, First-Served (FCFS)

FCFS is the simplest disk scheduling algorithm where requests are served in the order they arrive. While it follows a fair order of serving requests based on arrival time, it may result in poor performance when requests are scattered across the disk. This can lead to increased seek time, as the head moves continuously from one request to the next without considering their proximity to the current head position.

SCAN

SCAN is an elevator algorithm that reduces the average seek time by continuously moving the head in one direction and servicing requests along the way. This provides a more efficient order of serving requests compared to FCFS. However, SCAN may cause increased waiting time for requests located in the opposite direction of head movement, as it does not consider their proximity to the current head position.

Circular SCAN (C-SCAN)

C-SCAN is a variant of the SCAN algorithm that aims to provide a more uniform distribution of waiting time for requests. Instead of reversing direction, the head jumps to the other end of the disk and starts servicing requests again. While it eliminates the increased waiting time for requests located between the head position and the start of the disk, it may result in increased waiting time for requests in the opposite direction.

LOOK

LOOK is an improvement over the SCAN algorithm, as it changes direction when there are no more requests in the current direction. This reduces the average seek time compared to SCAN, as it avoids unnecessary movement to the end of the disk. LOOK provides a more efficient distribution of waiting time for requests, considering their proximity to the current head position.

Circular LOOK (C-LOOK)

C-LOOK is a variant of the LOOK algorithm that provides a more uniform distribution of waiting time for requests. Similar to C-SCAN, the head jumps to the other end of the disk when there are no more requests in the current direction. C-LOOK eliminates the unnecessary movement to the end of the disk, resulting in improved performance compared to LOOK.

Shortest Seek Time First (SSTF)

SSTF selects the request that is closest to the current head position, minimizing the seek time by choosing the shortest seek time for each request. It can result in a more optimal average seek time compared to other algorithms. However, SSTF may cause starvation for requests located further away from the current head position, as it always prioritizes the closest request.
